Belle: A Retrospective Look at Virtual Reality and the Future of Identity

Decades after its release, Mamoru Hosoda’s Belle remains a landmark work that questions our relationship with virtual reality and its impact on human identity. How has this vision evolved over time ?

Published on October 8, 2060 | By Vestiges VR |

Category: Film | 4 comments

Still from the film, Suzu connects to U for the first time

An Emotional Journey Between Two Worlds

When it was released in 2021, Belle told the story of Suzu, a shy young girl who transforms into a famous singer in the virtual world of "U". Hosoda imagined a future where virtual reality became a space for self-exploration, allowing users to redefine their identity and build genuine connections.

"In a technology-saturated world, virtual reality could be both a refuge and a mirror of our inner truths."– Mamoru Hosoda

Looking back, it’s fascinating to see how accurately this work predicted the challenges and opportunities virtual reality would bring. Hosoda captured the essence of our need for connection and acceptance in an increasingly digital environment.

Still from the film, Suzu in the virtual world of U

A Warning About the Risks and Pitfalls

However, Belle wasn’t just a celebration of virtual reality. The film also cautioned against the dangers of excessive immersion. Users could find an escape from their reality, but in doing so, they might face difficult truths.

Moreover, the issue of anonymity in a virtual world like U, where everyone is hidden behind avatars, presented both advantages and drawbacks, especially in how users behaved.

Decades later, the question remains: how far can virtual reality enhance our lives without alienating us from our humanity?

"Virtual reality was meant to enrich us, not alienate us."– Mamoru Hosoda
